# Voice Production & Vocal Registers (Chest, Head, Falsetto)

## 1. What is Voice Production?

Voice production refers to how sound is generated and shaped in the human body.  
It consists of **three core components**:

### 1.1 Breath Support (Respiration)
- Air comes from the lungs
- Controlled by diaphragm and rib cage
- Provides the **power source** for sound

### 1.2 Phonation (Vocal Cord Vibration)
- Vocal cords (vocal folds) vibrate as air passes through
- This creates the **basic sound wave**
- Key factor: **how well the vocal cords close**

### 1.3 Resonance (Sound Shaping)
- Sound is amplified and shaped in:
    - Chest
    - Throat
    - Mouth
    - Nasal cavity
- Determines **tone quality (timbre)**

---

## 2. Core Principle of Voice Production

👉 **Airflow + Vocal Cord Closure + Resonance = Final Sound**

More specifically:
- Strong airflow + good cord closure → strong, clear voice
- Weak closure → airy, weak voice
- Different resonance → different vocal color

---

## 3. Vocal Registers Overview

Vocal registers describe **how the vocal cords behave** and **where the sound resonates**.

We focus on:
- Chest Voice
- Head Voice
- Falsetto

---

## 4. Chest Voice

### 4.1 Voice Production Characteristics
- Vocal cords: **fully closed and thick**
- Air leakage: **minimal**
- Vibration: **strong and deep**
- Resonance: mainly in the **chest area**

### 4.2 Sound Features
- Strong, full, powerful
- Rich and heavy tone
- Used for low to mid range

### 4.3 Feeling
- Vibration in chest
- Solid and grounded

---

## 5. Head Voice

### 5.1 Voice Production Characteristics
- Vocal cords: **fully closed but thinner/stretched**
- Air leakage: **minimal**
- Vibration: **lighter but controlled**
- Resonance: **head / mask area**

### 5.2 Sound Features
- Clear, focused, and supported
- Lighter than chest voice but still strong
- Used for higher notes in a controlled way

### 5.3 Feeling
- Vibration in face/head
- Light but **not airy**

---

## 6. Falsetto

### 6.1 Voice Production Characteristics
- Vocal cords: **not fully closed (gap present)**
- Air leakage: **high**
- Vibration: **weak and incomplete**
- Resonance: head area, but inefficient

### 6.2 Sound Features
- Airy, breathy, soft
- Weak and unstable
- Often used for stylistic effect

### 6.3 Feeling
- Lots of air escaping
- Lack of support

---

## 7. Key Differences (Important)

| Feature          | Chest Voice        | Head Voice         | Falsetto            |
|-----------------|------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Cord Closure     | Full             | Full              | Partial (gap)       |
| Strength         | Strong           | Medium-Strong     | Weak                |
| Airiness         | None             | None              | High                |
| Tone             | Heavy / Rich     | Light / Clear     | Breathy / Thin      |
| Control          | High             | High              | Low                 |
| Usage            | Low–Mid notes    | Mid–High notes    | Stylistic / soft    |

---

## 8. Practical Insight for Singers

- Weak high notes → often **falsetto (too airy)**
- Goal → develop **head voice or mixed voice**
- Key training focus:
    - Improve **vocal cord closure**
    - Maintain **breath support**
    - Reduce unnecessary air leakage

---

## 9. Simple Mental Model

- Chest Voice = **Strong & Heavy**
- Head Voice = **Light but Supported**
- Falsetto = **Airy & Weak**

---

## 10. Conclusion

Voice production is not just about pitch —  
it is about **how efficiently your vocal system works**:

👉 Breath (power)  
👉 Vocal cords (source)  
👉 Resonance (shape)

Mastering the difference between chest voice, head voice, and falsetto  
is essential for building a **strong, controlled singing voice**.
